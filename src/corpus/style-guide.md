# Side Quest Syndicate — Style Guide

This guide covers tone, vocabulary, formatting, and structure for all Side Quest Syndicate editorial content. Writers should read this before their first piece and revisit it when in doubt. Editors enforce these rules at the copy level — not to homogenise voice, but to maintain quality and consistency.

## Tone of Voice

**Conversational authority.** We write like a knowledgeable friend, not a journalist performing objectivity. Use "I" when expressing an opinion. Use "we" when speaking as the publication. Never use the passive voice to avoid taking a position — if you think something is bad, say it is bad and explain why.

**Register.** Casual enough to be readable. Not so casual that it becomes sloppy. Contractions are fine. Slang is fine when it fits the context and the audience will understand it. Avoid corporate euphemisms ("content ecosystem," "player journey," "engagement surface"). Write like a person.

**Humor policy.** Humor is welcome when it serves the writing. Do not force it. A bad joke in the middle of a serious review breaks the reader's trust in your criticism. A well-timed observation can make a long review more enjoyable. When in doubt, cut the joke and keep the argument.

**Hedging.** Do not over-hedge opinions. "I think this is probably somewhat problematic in certain respects" is a waste of the reader's time. "The pacing collapses in the third act" is a sentence. Back it up with evidence, not additional hedges.

## Vocabulary Rules

**Gaming terminology to use freely.** Genre names (soulslike, metroidvania, roguelite), mechanic names (parry, hitbox, proc, cooldown, aggro), platform names (PS5, Xbox Series X, PC, Switch), and community-standard terms (NG+, DLC, patch notes, early access). Use these without explanation for the core audience.

**Terms requiring context.** When writing for guides or newcomer-accessible content, briefly gloss terms that are genre-specific rather than universal. "The boss has a tight parry window — parrying means pressing block at the exact moment an attack lands" is appropriate in a beginner guide. It is not appropriate in a review aimed at the core audience.

**Words to avoid.** "Masterpiece" — reserve for genuine once-in-a-generation work, and even then use sparingly. "Garbage" — too dismissive, replace with specific criticism. "Gamer" as a demographic — our audience plays games; the cultural "gamer" identity is contested and divisive. "Literally" when used for emphasis rather than accuracy. "Unique" — everything is unique in some sense; be specific about what is unusual or distinctive.

**Capitalisation.** Game titles are capitalised per their official stylisation. Genre names are not capitalised (soulslike not Soulslike, roguelite not Roguelite, unless beginning a sentence). Platform holder names follow their official capitalisation (PlayStation, Xbox, Nintendo, Steam).

## Sentence and Formatting Standards

**Sentence length.** Vary it. Long sentences build complexity and carry argument. Short sentences land points. The worst writing uses only one length — essays written in only short sentences feel like bullet points, and essays written in only long sentences become exhausting. Aim for rhythmic variation.

**Paragraph length.** Three to five sentences is a guideline, not a rule. A one-sentence paragraph is sometimes exactly right. Paragraphs of more than six sentences in digital publishing usually need to be broken up — readers are reading on screens and white space aids comprehension.

**Subheadings in long-form.** For pieces over 1,200 words, use subheadings. They are navigation aids for readers who skim before committing to the full read. Subheadings should be informative, not clever at the expense of clarity.

**Bold and emphasis.** Bold is for terms, key phrases, and genuinely critical information. It is not for decoration. Italics are for game titles (when not using title case styling in a format that does not support it), for stress on a specific word, and for internal thoughts in personal essays.

## Headline Formula

A good Side Quest Syndicate headline does one of the following:

1. States the verdict with specificity: "Avowed Is a Better Game Than Its Metacritic Score Suggests"
2. Poses the genuine question the piece answers: "Why Does Every Live Service Game End Up Feeling the Same?"
3. Names the tension: "Balatro Is Brilliant and I Resent How Much Time It Has Taken From Me"

Avoid: clickbait ambiguity ("You Won't Believe What This Game Does in Its Final Hour"), false urgency ("Why You Need to Play This Game Right Now"), and SEO stuffing in the headline (put the SEO term in the first paragraph instead).

## Do and Do Not Examples

**Do:** "The combat is satisfying because the parry window is forgiving enough to learn but tight enough to feel skilful once mastered."
**Do not:** "The combat is amazing and incredibly fun and you will love every second of it."

**Do:** "The game's economy makes no sense — after twelve hours I had more gold than I could spend, which drained all tension from the loot system."
**Do not:** "The economy system is somewhat lacking in certain respects and could potentially be improved."

**Do:** "I bounced off this game twice before it clicked. The third time, I finished it in a week and thought about it for a month."
**Do not:** "Some players may find this game difficult to get into at first but it gets better."

**Do:** "This is a bad port. The PC version runs at a locked 30fps with no option to uncap, and the controls have not been remapped for mouse and keyboard."
**Do not:** "Console players may have a slightly better experience than PC players in some technical areas."

**Do:** "Elden Ring's open world design solves a problem FromSoftware has never solved before: making exploration feel rewarding rather than punishing."
**Do not:** "Elden Ring is a masterpiece that revolutionises the open world genre in every conceivable way."

**Do:** Use "they" as a singular gender-neutral pronoun.
**Do not:** Use "he" as a generic singular. Use "she" or "he" only when referring to a specific person who uses that pronoun.
