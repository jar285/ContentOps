# Side Quest Syndicate — Content Calendar Template

The content calendar governs our weekly publishing cadence. It is a planning tool, not a cage — breaking it for a compelling piece is allowed, but departing from it should be a deliberate editorial decision, not a drift. This document covers the standard weekly cadence, content formats per slot, the approval workflow, and the first-week plan for new content operators.

## Weekly Cadence

Side Quest Syndicate publishes seven days a week at varying intensity. High-investment pieces are published Tuesday through Thursday when reader attention is highest. Monday and Friday are for lighter, community-forward content. Weekends run evergreen and community content that benefits from the slower browsing pace.

**Monday — Community and Conversation**
The week opens with a community-facing piece. This is the lowest editorial overhead day. Typical formats: "What Are You Playing This Week?" thread (Discord-first, then site post), community poll launch, or a short Discord digest from the previous week. Word count: 200–400 words. Goal: warm up the community for the week.

**Tuesday — Guide or Evergreen**
A practical, searchable piece. Guides published on Tuesday benefit from search indexing before the weekend when players have more time to use them. Typical formats: boss guide, system explainer, or build recommendation. Word count: 600–1,200 words. Goal: organic search traffic and Discord reference link ("check the guide we posted Tuesday").

**Wednesday — Review or Critical Coverage**
The editorial anchor of the week. Wednesday reviews reach the audience when they are making Thursday and Friday purchasing decisions before the weekend. For major releases, this slot is held for the embargo lift. For quieter weeks, this becomes a re-evaluation or a port report. Word count: 1,200–3,000 words. Goal: trust and authority.

**Thursday — News and Industry Commentary**
The mid-week news slot. Thursday commentary reaches readers before the weekend social media cycle. Typical formats: news brief with take, analysis piece on an industry development, or a response to a controversy that broke earlier in the week. Word count: 400–1,200 words. Goal: timely, opinionated coverage.

**Friday — Long-Form or Essay**
The premium reading slot. Friday long-form is for the piece we are most proud of that week — the essay, the investigation, the retrospective. Friday readers are often winding down and will commit time to a well-crafted piece. Word count: 1,500–3,500 words. Goal: reputation, sharing, newsletter pick-up.

**Saturday — Evergreen or Archive Spotlight**
A lighter publishing day. Saturday content is often a re-promotion of a strong archive piece, a short guide update, or a community feature. Word count: 300–600 words (or re-promotion). Goal: maintain publishing rhythm without burning out the team.

**Sunday — Newsletter Prep and Discord Wrap**
Sunday is primarily an internal day. The newsletter for Monday delivery is finalised. A Discord "week in review" post goes up in the afternoon. No new editorial content unless a major news story breaks. Word count: 0 (editorial) / newsletter send.

## Approval Workflow

All content passes through four stages before publication. No piece skips a stage.

**Stage 1: Pitch.** Writer submits a 2–3 sentence pitch in the editorial channel on Discord or via the shared pitch document. Pitch must include: the angle (not just the topic), the target pillar, and the intended publish slot. Editor reviews within 24 hours and approves, requests revision, or declines with a brief explanation.

**Stage 2: Draft.** Writer produces a complete draft. "Complete" means the piece has an intro, a body, a conclusion, and all claims are supported. Partial drafts are not submitted for review — this wastes the editor's time. Draft is submitted via the shared Google Doc with "DRAFT — [Title]" naming convention.

**Stage 3: Edit.** Editor completes a full editorial pass within 48 hours of draft submission. The edit covers structure, argument, voice, accuracy, and copy. Editor leaves comments in the document; writer addresses them. For contested edits, a brief synchronous conversation is preferred over long async threads.

**Stage 4: Publish.** Once the writer has addressed all editorial comments and the editor has approved the final version, the piece moves to the publishing queue. The editor or a designated content operator formats it in the CMS, attaches the correct tags (pillar, platform, game title), and schedules it for the target slot. The writer is notified when it goes live.

## First-Week Plan for New Content Operators

A new content operator joining the team runs a structured first week before taking on independent production. The goal is to build familiarity with the CMS, the calendar, and the voice before shipping original work.

**Day 1: Orientation.** Read the brand identity, style guide, content pillars, and audience profile documents in full. Take notes on anything unclear. Set up Discord access and CMS login. Attend or review the most recent editorial meeting notes.

**Day 2: Shadowing.** Observe the editor processing one piece through the full approval workflow. No independent action — watch and ask questions after. Review three published pieces across different pillars and identify where the style guide rules are applied.

**Day 3: First Pitch.** Submit one pitch following the Stage 1 process. The pitch can be for any pillar. The goal is to practise the pitch format and receive feedback on the angle, not necessarily to produce the piece immediately.

**Day 4: Draft Exercise.** Write a 400-word practice draft on a previously covered topic (no publication intention). The goal is to apply the style guide rules under zero-pressure conditions. Submit to the editor for annotated feedback.

**Day 5: First Production Assignment.** Take a confirmed editorial slot from the upcoming calendar — typically a Tuesday guide or a Monday community post. Produce the piece through all four approval stages with the editor's active support. This first published piece is the handshake moment: it signals readiness for independent operation.
